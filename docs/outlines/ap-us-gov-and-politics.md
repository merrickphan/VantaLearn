### Unit 1: Foundations of American Democracy

#### Enlightenment Philosophies
- Framers lived at a time when there were new ideas about government organization/function
- Challenged systems already in place
- Enlightenment (18th century): a philosophical movement that began in Western Europe with roots in Scientific Revolution
- Use of reason over tradition when solving social problems

##### Thomas Hobbes (Leviathan - 1660)
- Believed that people could not govern themselves and that a monarch with absolute power would protect life best
- Advocated for rule of law
- Social contract with government: some freedoms sacrificed (respecting government) in exchange for government protection

##### John Locke (Second Treatise on Civil Government - 1690)
- Natural rights must be protected
- Empiricism: people are born with a tabula rasa (blank slate) on equal footing and everything they do is shaped by experience
- Natural rights (life, liberty, property) are granted by God and government must protect them
- Right to revolution if natural rights are taken away

##### Charles de Montesquieu (The Spirit of the Laws - 1748)
- Separation of power into three branches of government
- Checks and balances limited power of each branch

##### Jean-Jacques Rousseau (The Social Contract - 1762)
- People are born good but corrupted by society
- Should act for the greater good rather than out of self-interest

##### Voltaire (Candide)
- Satirical novel, reflected dislike of Christian power and nobles
- Rationality; advocate of freedom of thought, speech, religion, and politics

##### Denis Diderot
- Producer/editor of first encyclopedia
- Wanted to change the ways people thought by adding his own/others’ philosophies
- Advocate of freedom of expression and universal education access
- Criticized divine right, traditional values, and religion

#### Philosophers favored democracy over absolute monarchy

##### Forms of representative democracy
- Participatory democracy: broad participation in politics/society by people at various statuses
- Pluralist democracy: group-based activism by citizens with common interests who seek the same goals
- Elite democracy: power to the educated/wealthy; discourages participation by the majority of people
- Republicanism: supports individualism and natural rights, popular sovereignty, encourages civic participation
- American Republicanism characterized by representative democracy:
  - Elected officials representing a group of people

#### Popular sovereignty
- Government power derives from the consent of the governed (e.g., elections, protests)

#### The Declaration of Independence
- Formal declaration of war between America and Great Britain
- Written by Thomas Jefferson
- List of grievances (“crimes” King George III committed against the colonies)
- Used to explain why the colonies are declaring independence
- Template for other nations declaring independence

#### Weaknesses of the Articles of Confederation
- First government of the United States; predecessor to the Constitution
- Followed from 1776 to 1781; ratified and named in 1781
- Accomplishments:
  - Created federalism: interaction and shared power between federal and state/regional governments
  - Ended the Revolutionary War on favorable terms (Treaty of Paris - 1783)
  - Established the Northwest Ordinance (methods for states to enter the U.S.)
- Weaknesses:
  - 1787: trade between states declined; monetary value dropped; foreign threats; social disorder
  - Shays’ Rebellion (1786-1787): farmers attacked a federal arsenal in protest of foreclosures in western Massachusetts
  - Could not impose taxes; only states could levy taxes
  - National debt from Revolutionary War; no reliable revenue
  - No national military; could not draft soldiers
  - No national currency
  - No Supreme Court to interpret law
  - No executive branch to enforce laws
  - No control over taxes between states; could not control interstate trade
  - Unanimous vote required to amend Articles
  - 9/13 states required to pass legislation
  - Could not control states; no enforcement mechanisms
- Led to Constitutional Convention and complete rewrite into the Constitution

#### The Constitutional Convention
- 1787 meeting in Philadelphia
- Division over powers/structure/responsibilities
- Generally pragmatists protecting property + rights; stronger central government necessary but potentially corruptible
- Legislature debates:
  - Unicameral vs bicameral
  - Virginia Plan (Madison): bicameral based on population
  - New Jersey Plan: unicameral; one vote per state
  - Great Compromise: House by population; Senate equal representation
- Representation of enslaved people:
  - Three-Fifths Compromise: enslaved people counted as 3/5 for House seats
- Enforcement:
  - Created chief executive (president); veto power; Congress can override veto with 2/3 of both houses
- Supreme Court:
  - Mediate disputes; between branches; between states; between state and federal

#### Acceptance of the Constitution
- Ratification by states
- Federalists supported strong central government
  - Hamilton, Madison, Jay wrote Federalist Papers (argued for Constitution; reflects framers’ intent)
- Anti-Federalists opposed; preferred smaller state governments (Articles)
  - Feared tyranny; wanted Bill of Rights
- Bill of Rights: 10 amendments written by James Madison; added soon after ratification
- Electoral College:
  - 538 electors; each state = 2 + House seats
  - Intended to buffer against uninformed choice / small-group influence; balance large vs small states
  - 270 wins

#### Federalist Papers and Anti-Federalist Dissent
- Brutus No. 1: critiqued Constitution; feared national power, standing army, non-representative reps
- Federalist No. 10 (Madison): factions; large republic prevents any one faction dominating
- Federalist No. 51 (Madison): separation of powers; “ambition must be made to counteract ambition”
- Federalist No. 70 (Hamilton): single executive; checked; term limits later (22nd Amendment)
- Federalist No. 78 (Hamilton): judiciary least powerful; judicial review; concerns about lifetime appointments

#### Constitution as instrument of government
- Vague; structured for change via amendments
- Articles I–III create branches
- Executive orders and executive agreements (not in Constitution) developed via practice
- Marbury v. Madison (1803): judicial review
- Necessary and Proper Clause (elastic clause): allows legislation needed to carry powers
- Supremacy Clause: Constitution and federal laws supreme over state laws

#### Federalism
- Shared powers between national and local governments
- Key cases:
  - McCulloch v. Maryland (1819): states can’t tax national bank; supremacy + necessary/proper
  - United States v. Lopez (1995): limits commerce clause; emphasizes state sovereignty

#### Powers under federalism
- Delegated/enumerated: money, commerce, treaties, war, post offices, courts, etc.
- Reserved (10th): licensing, intrastate business, elections, local gov, education, health/safety/welfare, etc.
- Concurrent: taxes, roads, courts, eminent domain, borrowing, etc.
- Denied powers listed for federal and state governments
- Grants-in-aid:
  - Categorical grants (strict; federal power)
  - Block grants (flexible; states’ rights)
- Pros/cons of federalism listed (participation, autonomy, innovation vs inconsistency, inefficiency, inequity)

#### Separation of powers; checks and balances
- Legislative makes laws; executive enforces; judicial interprets
- Examples: confirmations, treaties, legislation/veto/override, judicial review

#### Amendment process
- Proposal: 2/3 both houses; ratification: 3/4 states (or conventions)
- Convention route: 2/3 states petition (never used)

#### State and local governments
- Governors: agencies, National Guard, pardons, veto, line-item veto (states)
- State legislatures: mostly bicameral; make laws; override veto
- State judiciaries: trial + appeals
