## AP European History — outline (user-provided)

### Unit 1: Renaissance and Exploration

#### 1.1: Context of the Renaissance

##### The Renaissance
- Period of cultural and intellectual rebirth beginning in Italy (14th century) and spreading through Europe until the 17th century
- Renewed interest in classical learning, humanism, and the arts

##### Humanism
- Philosophical/intellectual movement emphasizing value and agency of human beings
- Reaction to medieval scholasticism
- Emphasized education, reason, critical thinking
- Studied classical literature, history, art

##### Art
- Renewed interest in classical forms, realism, humanism
- Leonardo da Vinci, Michelangelo, Raphael
- Perspective, chiaroscuro, sfumato for depth/realism
- Printing technology disseminated art/ideas

##### Science
- Scientific discovery and innovation
- Galileo Galilei, Johannes Kepler contributions to astronomy/physics
- Scientific method emphasized observation, experimentation, empirical evidence

##### Literature
- Classical forms, humanism, vernacular
- Shakespeare, Cervantes, Dante
- Explored human nature, morality, human condition
- Printing press spread literature and ideas

##### European society at the time
- Artistic/cultural flourishing; scientific advancements
- Rise of nation-states and centralized power
- Protestant Reformation and religious conflicts
- Exploration/colonization of the New World
- Emergence of middle class and capitalism
- Patriarchal society with limited rights for women
- Slavery and exploitation of non-European peoples
- Plague outbreaks and public health concerns

##### What led to explorations?
- Crusades and desire for new trade routes to Asia
- Renaissance revival of classical knowledge
- Navigation/shipbuilding advances
- Competition among European nations for wealth/power
- Columbus’s discovery of the New World

#### 1.2: Italian Renaissance
- Cultural/artistic growth (14th–17th centuries)
- Classical art/literature/philosophy; humanism; individualism
- Emergence factors: rediscovery of Greek/Roman texts, wealthy merchant classes, patronage (e.g., Medici)
- Impact: new artistic techniques (perspective, chiaroscuro); major influence on Western culture
- Works: Mona Lisa, Last Supper; David; Sistine Chapel ceiling; School of Athens; Divine Comedy; Petrarch’s sonnets; Decameron

#### 1.3: Northern Renaissance
- Northern Europe (15th–16th centuries): classical learning, individualism, humanism
- Characteristics: humanism; Reformation; realism/detail; light/shadow; scientific advancements
- Figures: Dürer, Jan van Eyck, Erasmus, Shakespeare

#### 1.4: Printing Press
- Gutenberg mid-15th century; revolutionized dissemination of information
- Books cheaper/more common → literacy and spread of ideas
- Disseminated: Bible, Shakespeare, Galileo, Luther

#### 1.5: New Monarchies (1450–1648)
- Centralization, bureaucracies, armies, national economies
- Factors: decline of feudalism; growth of trade/merchant class; Renaissance; Reformation
- Examples: France (Louis XI, Francis I, Henry IV), Spain (Isabella/Ferdinand, Charles I), England (Henry VII, Henry VIII, Elizabeth I), Russia (Ivan III, Ivan IV), Portugal (John II)

#### 1.6: Age of Exploration
- 15th–17th centuries exploration for routes/resources/territories
- Causes: trade, religion, technology, wealth
- Explorers: Columbus, Vasco da Gama, Magellan, Francis Drake
- Consequences: colonization; global trade/capitalism; cultural exchange; exploitation/slavery

#### 1.7: Colonial Rivals
- Expansion effects: displacement/enslavement; suppression of cultures; tech/religion/political systems; resource extraction; European economic growth
- Rivalries: Treaty of Tordesillas; Anglo-French rivalry (French and Indian War); Scramble for Africa (late 19th c.); contributed to WWI

#### 1.8: Columbian Exchange
- Exchange of plants/animals/diseases Old ↔ New Worlds post-1492
- Old World: new crops; precious metals; disease to Americas (smallpox)
- New World: horses/cattle; guns/iron tools; disease devastation

#### 1.9: Slave Trade and Triangular Trade
- Began 15th century (Portuguese); expanded with sugar plantations; other European powers joined
- Triangular trade: manufactured goods → Africa; enslaved Africans → Americas; cash crops/raw materials → Europe
- Abolition in 19th century; lasting legacy

#### 1.10: Commercial Revolution
- 16th–18th centuries: economic expansion, colonialism, mercantilism
- Effects: trade growth; capitalism; banking; joint-stock companies; inflation/price rises
- Social: urbanization; middle class; consumer culture; colonial expansion/exploitation

#### 1.11: Causation in Renaissance and Age of Discovery
- Renaissance laid groundwork for Discovery; Discovery fed patronage and change; shaped by political/economic/social forces

### Unit 2: Age of Reformation

#### 2.1: Contextualizing 16th and 17th-Century Challenges and Developments

##### Pre-Luther Reformers and Reform Movements
- John Wycliffe: vernacular Bible; criticized wealth/corruption; Lollards
- Jan Hus: criticized indulgences/corruption; vernacular services; executed 1415
- Erasmus: humanist; criticized corruption; reason/classical texts; influenced reform
- Savonarola: criticized corruption/immorality; called for moral Christianity; executed 1498

##### Critiques of the Catholic Church
- Corruption (incl. indulgences)
- Clerical celibacy abuses
- Papal authority criticized; papal infallibility criticized
- Sale of church offices
- Latin services alienated ordinary people

##### Economic developments
- New World + Asia trade routes increased trade/commerce
- Rise of capitalism + merchant class; new banking/finance (e.g., Dutch banking)
- Colonialism; exploitation of resources/labor

##### Political developments
- Rise of nation-states; centralized monarchies; decline of feudalism
- Religious conflicts; Thirty Years’ War; Peace of Westphalia; state sovereignty
- Absolutism (Louis XIV as exemplar)
- Colonialism expansion and competition
- Enlightenment emerges late 17th century (reason, rights, separation of powers)

#### 2.2: Luther and the Protestant Reformation
- Martin Luther: dissatisfaction with Catholic practices (indulgences); 95 Theses (1517); faith + Bible; rejects papal authority; German Bible translation; spread and denominational diversity; impacts wars/politics/capitalism rise (per outline)
- John Calvin: Institutes; predestination; disciplined church; Geneva theocracy; influence on Protestantism

##### Protestant beliefs (as listed)
- Sola Scriptura; Sola Fide; Sola Gratia
- Priesthood of all believers
- Justification by faith
- Trinity
- Two sacraments (baptism, Lord’s Supper)
- Reject separate priestly class; church as body of believers
- Second coming

#### 2.3: Protestant Reform Continues
- Priesthood of all believers shifted authority from Church to state in many areas; creation of state churches (e.g., Church of England)
- State control consolidated power but caused conflict/persecution
- Groups opposing state: Anabaptists; Radical reformers; peasants; Protestant dissenters; Waldensians

#### 2.4: Wars of Religion
- German Peasants’ War (1524–25); Schmalkaldic Wars (1546–47); Peace of Augsburg (1555) “cuius regio, eius religio”
- French Wars of Religion (1562–1598); Massacre of Vassy (1562); St. Bartholomew’s Day (1572); Edict of Nantes (1598)
- Thirty Years’ War (1618–1648): brutality; Peace of Westphalia; Dutch Republic + Switzerland recognized; religious freedom for Protestants (per outline)
- Manipulative states used Reformation for political agendas

#### 2.5: The Catholic Reformation (Counter-Reformation)
- Causes: Protestant challenge; printing press spread; corruption critiques
- Figures: Pope Paul III; Ignatius of Loyola (Jesuits); Teresa of Avila
- Events: Council of Trent (1545–1563); Jesuits (1540); Teresa reforms; seminaries; indulgences banned
- Impact: regained followers; clarified teachings; Baroque renewal

#### 2.6: 16th-Century Society & Politics in Europe
- Social ladder: nobility, clergy, bourgeoisie, peasants; women limited rights
- Community/leisure: villages; church-centered; festivals/fairs/sports; hunting (nobility)

#### 2.7: Mannerism and Baroque Art
- Mannerism: distorted proportions, exaggerated poses, artificial colors; figures noted in outline (El Greco, Pontormo, Rosso Fiorentino, Parmigianino; Caravaggio as Baroque pioneer)
- Baroque: dramatic light/shadow; opulence; emotion; movement; allegory/symbolism; Bernini, Rubens, Rembrandt, Velázquez, Caravaggio

#### 2.8: Causation in Reformation and Wars of Religion
- Religious dissatisfaction and Protestant challenge → tensions/wars
- Political ambitions of rulers; wars often political
- Social/economic unrest; devastating local impacts
